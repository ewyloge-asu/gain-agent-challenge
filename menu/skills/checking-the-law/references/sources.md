# Where to read the law — trusted sources, any domain

Which sources to trust for legal text, tiered. **Only Tier 1 is the law itself**; everything below
helps you find, interpret, or contextualize it. Always end on a Tier 1 primary source, quote it,
and record the URL + the date you read it. Laws are amended — confirm currency and note
effective-date/amendment notes. (US-centric list; for other jurisdictions use the official
gazette / consolidated-legislation site and that country's enforcing agency, same tiers.)

## Tier 1 — Primary law (quote only from here)
- **Federal statutes:** Cornell LII `law.cornell.edu/uscode` (free, hyperlinked definitions —
  best default) · `uscode.house.gov` (the official U.S. Code — confirm currency and exact
  subsection lettering) · `govinfo.gov` (official publisher: U.S. Code, Statutes at Large,
  historical versions).
- **Federal regulations:** current CFR at **eCFR** `ecfr.gov`; new/proposed rules at
  `federalregister.gov`.
- **Enacted bills & legislative history:** `congress.gov`.
- **State law:** the state legislature's official code site (search "<state> revised statutes" /
  "<state> code official"); Justia (`law.justia.com/codes`) and FindLaw mirror state codes for
  navigation — verify the text on the official site. State regulations: the state's
  administrative code. Local ordinances: Municode (`library.municode.com`) / American Legal.
- **Case law** (when a court's reading controls a term): `supremecourt.gov`, `uscourts.gov`,
  **CourtListener** `courtlistener.com` (free), Justia — read the opinion's holding, not a summary.

## Tier 2 — The enforcing body's official guidance (how the law is actually applied)
Authoritative *interpretation* by whoever enforces the provision — cite as guidance, never as the
statute. **Find it by asking "who would punish this?"** (see finding-the-law.md): that body's site
has a "laws we enforce" page, manuals, FAQs, and advisory opinions. Cross-domain examples: an
election commission's advisory opinions · a securities regulator's rules/releases · an
environmental agency's guidance and permit databases · a labor department's opinion letters · a
legislature's own ethics-committee manuals · an inspector general's or filing office's official
guidance for a disclosure regime. (For this bundle's beat, the specific offices and links live in
the example pack: [packs/us-influence-law.md](packs/us-influence-law.md).)

## Tier 3 — Nonpartisan analysis (orientation before you read)
- **Congressional Research Service** `crsreports.congress.gov` — neutral, citation-dense topic
  reports; often the fastest map to a provision's scope.
- **GAO** `gao.gov` — oversight and how-programs-actually-work reports.
- Official law-revision notes and LII's **Wex** encyclopedia for doctrine names.

## Tier 4 — Pointers only (never cite as the law)
Law-firm client alerts and blogs, advocacy/watchdog explainers, Wikipedia, Ballotpedia. Useful for
*finding* a citation or knowing what question to ask; always verify in Tier 1. Watchdogs tell you
where to look; they don't tell you what the law says.

## How to work a provision once you have it
1. Get the citation (finding-the-law.md; a domain pack if you have one; a CRS report; or a
   targeted `site:law.cornell.edu <topic>` search).
2. Open the Tier 1 text and **read the definitions section first** — most questions turn on a
   defined term ("covered person," "contribution," "point source," "accredited investor").
3. Read the specific subsection AND its exceptions, thresholds, and scope limits — that is where
   "looks illegal" usually dies.
4. Check amendment/effective-date notes to confirm you have the current version.
5. Quote the operative sentence verbatim in your output, with the URL and as-of date.
