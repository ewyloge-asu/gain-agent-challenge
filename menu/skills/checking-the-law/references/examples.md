# Worked examples — three legal checks, three domains

Complete runs of the method in the exact output format, each chosen to teach a different lesson:
scope limits (influence), definitions + enforcement databases (environment), and state-of-mind
limits (securities). Facts are generic composites; the statutes are real — always re-read the
current text before relying on any citation here.

---

## Example 1 (influence / post-employment): the cooling-off ban that wasn't violated
**Facts:** A counsel to a Congressional committee leaves in April; five months later she is a
registered lobbyist contacting House offices for a trade association whose agenda (transport,
banking) is unrelated to her old committee's jurisdiction. The lead: "she's lobbying within her
one-year cooling-off period — illegal?"

```
LEGAL CHECK  Does the ex-committee counsel's House lobbying within a year violate the
             post-employment ban?
provision:   18 U.S.C. § 207(e) (legislative-branch post-employment restrictions), esp. (e)(4)
             for committee employees   (read at law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/207, as-of <date>)
elements:    [ covered former role — MET: committee employee over the pay threshold ]
             [ lobbying CONTACT (not mere employment/registration) — MET: she contacts offices ]
             [ within 1 year of departure — MET: five months ]
             [ contact within the ban's SCOPE — NOT MET: for a former committee employee the ban
               reaches only her former committee's members and staff; her contacts are unrelated
               House offices ]
scope/limits: § 207(e)'s reach differs sharply by role — members (broad, 1–2 yr), senior personal
             staff (their office/chamber), committee staff (that committee only). The ban also
             restricts contacts, never the act of registering or behind-the-scenes work.
verdict:     not-a-violation
reasoning:   Every element of the prohibition is satisfied except scope: a committee employee's
             one-year ban covers only the committee she served. Contacting other offices on
             unrelated matters is outside the restriction, however fast the revolving door spun.
would-change: Any documented contact with her former committee's members or staff inside the year.
for-the-panel: none — cleared. The finding reframes to a legal revolving-door pattern (speed +
             seniority), not a violation.
```
**Lessons:** the scope limit was the whole answer; "looks illegal" died in subsection (e)(4); the
cleared check *improved* the story by making it publishable without a false accusation.

## Example 2 (environment): the pipe in the creek
**Facts:** Residents photograph a factory outfall pipe discharging colored effluent into a
year-round creek that feeds a river. The lead: "they're dumping into the creek — illegal?"

```
LEGAL CHECK  Is the facility's discharge into the creek unlawful?
provision:   Clean Water Act — 33 U.S.C. § 1311(a) (unlawful discharge except in compliance),
             § 1342 (NPDES permits), definitions at § 1362(6),(7),(12),(14)
             (read at law.cornell.edu, as-of <date>)
elements:    [ addition of a POLLUTANT (§1362(6)) — LIKELY MET: industrial effluent qualifies ]
             [ from a POINT SOURCE (§1362(14)) — MET: a discrete pipe is the textbook case ]
             [ to WATERS OF THE UNITED STATES (§1362(7)) — LIKELY MET for a relatively permanent
               creek connected to a river; NOTE this term is fiercely litigated (Sackett v. EPA
               (2023) narrowed coverage for wetlands) — verify the water's status ]
             [ WITHOUT or in violation of an NPDES permit — UNKNOWN: must check. Permits and
               violation histories are public — EPA's ECHO database (echo.epa.gov) and the state
               permit registry list the facility's permit, limits, and exceedances ]
scope/limits: A permit CONVERTS the analysis: discharge per a valid permit is lawful; the story
             becomes whether they exceeded permit limits (also in ECHO) or the permit is lax.
verdict:     genuinely-unclear — pending the permit lookup (which is a records step, not a legal one)
reasoning:   Three elements are satisfied on their face; legality turns entirely on permit status
             and compliance, which the enforcer's own database answers.
would-change: ECHO showing no permit (→ possible-violation) or exceedances (→ possible-violation
             of permit terms) or full compliance (→ not-a-violation; possible lax-permit story).
for-the-panel: none yet — complete the ECHO/state lookup first.
```
**Lessons:** definitions are battlegrounds (know when a term is litigated); the enforcing agency's
*database* is often the missing element; a permit flips the question from "may they?" to "did they
stay within it?".

## Example 3 (securities): the well-timed trades
**Facts:** Disclosure records show an executive's relative sold a large position days before bad
news tanked the stock. The lead: "insider trading?"

```
LEGAL CHECK  Do the relative's pre-announcement sales constitute unlawful insider trading?
provision:   15 U.S.C. § 78j(b) and SEC Rule 10b-5 (17 C.F.R. § 240.10b-5), as construed by
             Dirks v. SEC (tippee liability requires the tipper's breach for personal benefit)
             and United States v. O'Hagan (misappropriation)   (read as-of <date>)
elements:    [ trade in a security — MET: documented sales ]
             [ while in possession of MATERIAL NONPUBLIC information — UNKNOWN: timing is
               suggestive; possession must be shown, not inferred from timing alone ]
             [ information obtained via a duty-breaching tip, for the tipper's personal benefit
               (Dirks) — UNKNOWN from public records ]
             [ SCIENTER (intent/knowledge) — UNKNOWN: the element public documents almost never
               prove ]
scope/limits: Suspicious timing is where SEC investigations START, not where liability ends; a
             pre-existing Rule 10b5-1 trading plan is a recognized defense — check filings for one.
verdict:     genuinely-unclear — objective facts (relationship, timing, size) are established;
             the knowledge elements are not provable from records
reasoning:   The documentable pattern satisfies none of the knowledge elements on its own. The
             honest story is the verified timing pattern plus the unanswered questions — not an
             accusation.
would-change: Evidence of communication about the news before the trades; absence/presence of a
             10b5-1 plan; an SEC filing or action.
for-the-panel: whether the timing pattern merits referral language ("records show trades preceding
             the announcement; experts say such patterns typically draw regulatory review").
```
**Lessons:** state-of-mind elements cap what records can prove — the verdict vocabulary protects
you; safe harbors (10b5-1) must be checked before publishing suspicion; write the facts, question
the legality.
