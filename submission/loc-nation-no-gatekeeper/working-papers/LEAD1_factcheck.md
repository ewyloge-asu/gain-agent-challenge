# Fact-check pass — every number, how it's counted, and where it's soft

*I went back at each figure adversarially, the way a hostile fact-checker would. Verdicts below:
two figures are rock-solid, one needs a small denominator caveat, one claim must be reworded, and
one (the blank-filings rate) was overstated and I'm correcting it to the honest number now.*

Quick scorecard:

| Figure | Verdict |
|---|---|
| $80 million | **Solid — use as is** (just call it "reported/claimed," not "spent") |
| "Biggest client in America" | **Reword** — true only by one measure; fix the framing |
| Distortion percentage | **Add the denominator** — 2.78% is income-only; all-in it's ~1.3% |
| Blank-filings rate ("a third") | **Overstated — correct it** to ~25% (and ~10% of activity reports) |

## 1. The $80 million — SOLID

**How it's counted:** Loc Nation filed a Senate report for each quarter of 2025 stating $20,000,000
of income. Counting **one figure per quarter** (the amendment-aware method — take the latest/highest
value per quarter so amendments aren't double-counted): 4 × $20,000,000 = **$80,000,000**. The entity
reports **no** "expenses" figure on any filing, so there's no alternate number in play.

**Could it be challenged?** Only on wording. The raw file, if you naïvely sum every amendment and the
mirrored House copies, reaches $180M — so anyone who wants to attack us would say "$180M is inflated."
We already avoided that by using the conservative **$80M**. The one discipline to keep: always say the
entity **"reported"** or **"claimed"** $80M, never that it **"spent"** or **"was paid"** $80M — no
money changed hands; it's a self-declared figure. **Verdict: stands. Frame as reported/claimed.**

---

## 2. "The biggest lobbying client in America" — REWORD (this one would get caught)

**How it's counted / the problem:** Lobbying filings carry two different dollar fields — **income**
(fees a client pays an outside lobbying firm) and **expenses** (what an organization spends lobbying
**in-house**). Loc Nation is #1 *only by income*. When you look at **in-house expenses**, real
organizations are in the same range or bigger:

- By **income**, 2025: Loc Nation **$80.0M**, then AIPAC $3.76M, Nippon Steel $3.42M. (Loc is ~21×
  the next-largest — that part is real and striking.)
- By **expenses**, 2025: **U.S. Chamber of Commerce $70.3M**, National Association of Realtors
  $54.1M, PhRMA $37.9M, Business Roundtable $33.5M, Meta $26.3M.

So a flat "biggest lobbying client in America" is **not defensible** — the Chamber and others spend
comparable **real** money. **The honest, still-powerful version:** *"By reported income — the fee a
client pays outside lobbying firms — the fabricated entity outranks every real company and trade
group in the country, more than 20 times the next-largest. Its claimed $80 million even exceeds the
single largest real lobbying budget in America, the U.S. Chamber of Commerce's roughly $70 million."*
That's checkable and lands harder because the comparison is to a real heavyweight. **Verdict: drop
"biggest client"; use the income-rank + Chamber comparison instead.**

## 3. The distortion percentage — ADD THE DENOMINATOR

**How it's counted:** Loc's $80M over total 2025 reported dollars. But "total" depends on which field:

- Total **income** (firm fees), 2025 = **$2.88B** → Loc = **2.78%**.
- Total **expenses** (in-house), 2025 = **$3.37B**.
- Total **activity** (income + expenses), 2025 = **$6.25B** → Loc = **1.28%**.

**Could it be challenged?** Yes, two ways. (a) "2.78% of all lobbying" is wrong — it's 2.78% of the
*income* slice only; of **all** reported lobbying dollars it's **1.28%**. (b) These totals are my own
computation from the corpus with an amendment-dedup rule, and they run higher than commonly published
figures (e.g., OpenSecrets reports ~$4–4.5B/yr using its own dedup) — so I should **not** present a
precise percentage as if it were an official statistic. **The honest version:** *"one fabricated
filer accounts for well over 1% of all reported federal lobbying dollars in 2025 — about 1.3% of the
income-plus-expenses total, or 2.8% if you look only at the client-fee side — on figures computed
from the raw filings."* Lead with the conservative **~1.3%**. **Verdict: keep, but state the
denominator and lead with the all-in number.**

## 4. The blank-filings rate — OVERSTATED; CORRECTING NOW

This is the one we were saying too strongly. We reported **"a third (34–36%) of filings are blank."**

**What that 35% actually is:** it counts filings where the **income** field is null. But in-house
lobbyists legitimately fill in **expenses instead of income** — so an "income-null" filing often
isn't blank at all. Counting filings with **no dollar figure in *either* field**:

- Truly-blank (income **and** expenses both null): **~25%** each year (24–26%, 2022–2025) — not 35%.
- And a big share of *that* is **legitimately** dollarless: **registrations (RR)** — ~6,900 filings a
  year — carry no dollar amount by design, and "no-activity" quarterly reports legitimately report
  none either.
- If you look only at **quarterly activity reports (Q1–Q4)**, the share reporting **neither** income
  nor expenses is about **10–12%** every year (2025: 10%).

**Honest multi-year table (recomputed):**

| Year | income-null (what we said) | both-null (truly no figure) | activity-reports-only, both-null |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2022 | 36% | 26% | 12% |
| 2023 | 36% | 25% | 11% |
| 2024 | 35% | 24% | 11% |
| 2025 | 35% | 25% | 10% |

**Verdict:** the "a third of the ledger is blank" line is **not defensible** and should be dropped.
The honest, still-useful statement: *"About a quarter of all filings carry no dollar figure at all —
though some of that is legitimate (registrations and no-activity reports). Even among quarterly
activity reports, roughly one in ten (10–12%) report neither income nor expenses — and that rate is
strikingly stable year to year."* The **stability** is the real point, and it survives. But note this
is now a **supporting** detail, not a pillar — the two solid pillars of the "no gatekeeper" story are
(a) the fabrication itself sailing through, and (b) the enforcement record.

## 5. The enforcement numbers — SOLID (sourced to GAO), historical examples flagged

**How they're counted:** these come straight from the government's own auditor, **GAO-25-107523
(2024 Lobbying Disclosure audit)**, quoting U.S. Attorney's Office data — I read the report directly:

- **3,566** referrals for failing to file LD-2 reports + **2,000** for failing to file LD-203 reports,
  cumulatively **2015–2024** — all for **non-filing**. *(GAO Figures 11–12.)*
- GAO's own wording: enforcement actions "**include letters, emails, and telephone calls**"; **36%**
  (1,290) of LD-2 referrals came into compliance, **63%** (2,244) still pending.
- **2024:** exactly **one** civil action — a **$65,000** settlement against a "chronic offender" —
  and **zero** criminal actions.
- **No** JACK Act prosecutions ever.

**Could it be challenged?** The primary 2015–2024 figures and the 2024 snapshot are airtight (direct
GAO quotes). Two disciplines: (1) the **pre-2015 settlement examples** ($45k/2011, $200k/2013,
$125k/2015) are from older reports and law-firm write-ups — **verify each against its GAO source
before printing a dollar figure**. (2) "None ever for false content" is a **negative** — say "no
documented case," and confirm via DOJ's **§ 1605(b)** semiannual reports and on the record with USAO.
**Verdict: stands, with those two caveats already in the enforcement memo.**

## Net effect on the story

Nothing here breaks the story — but it makes it **tighter and unimpeachable**:

- **Keep, unchanged:** the $80M fabrication (reported), that it's ~21× the largest real client by
  income and tops even the U.S. Chamber's ~$70M real budget, and the GAO enforcement record.
- **Reword:** "biggest client in America" → the income-rank + Chamber comparison.
- **Correct:** distortion is ~1.3% all-in (not 2.78% of everything); blank rate is ~25% (with much of
  it legitimate) and ~10% among activity reports — not "a third."
- **Reframe:** the blank-data point drops from a pillar to a supporting detail; the story now rests on
  the two things that can't be argued down — the fabrication got in, and almost nothing happens after.

The two headline sentences I'd stand behind after this pass:

> A single filer registered a fictitious "sovereign nation" and reported **$80 million** in federal
> lobbying fees for 2025 — more than 20 times any real client by that measure, and more than the
> largest real in-house lobbying budget in the country — and it posted to the official record with
> nothing checking it.

> The government's own auditor shows the disclosure law is enforced almost entirely against people who
> **fail to file**, mostly with letters and calls — a single $65,000 civil settlement in 2024, no
> criminal prosecutions — with **no documented case** of anyone ever penalized for filing a false
> figure.
