# The "in honor of" money — findings summary (Lead 2)

**Investigation:** Beyond capped campaign donations, companies give money "in honor of" politicians — to
charities, foundations, buildings, and events tied to them. Map who gives, how much, and where it goes; find
where it clusters; and pressure-test the sharpest lead against real outside records and the actual law.
**Status:** Documented from records; the headline lead reconciled against the authoritative state record and
checked element-by-element against the statute. Every number below was re-derived in a final verification
pass. Ready for reporting-out (comment + lawyer review) before publication.
**Framing rule (non-negotiable):** this money is *disclosed and legal on its face*, and it goes to charities
and entities, not into politicians' pockets. Nothing here asserts anyone broke a law. The story is the
*channel*, where it *concentrates*, and one place where the federal disclosure record is conspicuously thin.

---

## The story in plain terms

There are two ways for corporate money to reach a politician's world. One is the campaign-contribution lane,
which is **capped** and heavily watched. The other is quieter: a company can give money **"in honor of"** a
member of Congress — paying for a gala that honors them, donating to a foundation or a building named for
them — and the law does **not cap** how much. It only asks that the money be *disclosed* on a lobbying form.

Across 2022 through early 2026, lobbying filers disclosed **$134 million** in this "honorary" lane. Most of it
is unremarkable and flows to well-known caucus charities. But following the single sharpest thread — a state
party building fund named the **"Mitch McConnell Building"** — turned up something worth a closer look. Over
three years, corporations and other interests put **$4.4 million** into that building. When you line those
donors up against their *own* federal lobbying reports, a clear split appears: **a few disclosed the gift, and
most did not** — even though they file these very reports all the time. Whether they were *required* to
disclose it comes down to one genuinely unsettled reading of the statute. That makes it a strong question for
a lawyer and for the companies — not a proven violation.

## Part 1 — The channel, mapped

**Scale.** 4,291 "Honorary Expenses" items (the `he` type on semiannual LD-203 reports), **$134,448,698
reported**, 2022 through 2026 Q1, running a steady ~$30–38M a year.

**Who gives.** The named "contributor" is just "SELF" on **93.1%** of items — the real giver is the filing
company. The biggest corporate givers: **Amazon $7.16M, State Farm $5.94M, Meta $5.40M, AARP $3.86M, Comcast
$3.44M, Toyota $3.43M.**

**Where it goes — the money concentrates in caucus charities:**

| Destination cluster | Reported | Distinct corporate funders |
|---|---:|---:|
| Congressional Black Caucus Foundation | $25.5M | 101 |
| Congressional Hispanic Caucus Institute + Leadership Institute | $22.7M | 80 |
| Asian Pacific American Institute for Congressional Studies (APAICS) | $5.0M | 39 |

*(A separate, non-"in honor of a member" layer also exists and isn't part of this story: Presidential
Inaugural Committee giving ~$67.6M and Presidential Library giving ~$22.4M.)*

**The single largest honorary item in the whole corpus** is State Farm's **$2,650,000 to Habitat for Humanity
"in honor of Rep. Virginia Foxx"** (2025-11-20). It's real — State Farm publicly renewed its ~$2.6M Habitat
partnership in June 2025 — and the notable thing is only that routine corporate philanthropy is filed on a
*lobbying* report tagged to one sitting member. That's a question for the company, not a violation.

## Part 2 — The full reconciliation: the "Mitch McConnell Building" fund

Kentucky's 2017 law lets a state party run a "building fund" that can take **unlimited corporate money** for
its headquarters — money that can't legally go to the party itself. The Republican Party of Kentucky's
headquarters is signed the **"Mitch McConnell Building."** The authoritative record of who funded it is the
fund's own filings with the **Kentucky Registry of Election Finance**, tracked by the Kentucky Lantern through
the project's completion (final accounting Jan 7, 2026): **~$4.43 million from about 22 named donors** plus 16
small gifts.

Lined up against each donor's **own federal LD-203 lobbying reports** (searched across all years for any
honorary building-fund item), the donors fall into three groups:

**A. Disclosed the gift federally — 4 donors, $1,475,000**

| Donor | State record | Federal LD-203 (honorary) |
|---|---:|---|
| Pfizer | $1,000,000 | $1,000,000 (2022) |
| AT&T | $300,000 | $300,000 ($100k ×3, 2022–24) |
| Microsoft | $125,000 | $125,000 (2023 + 2025) |
| Delta | $50,000 | disclosed — shown **twice** federally ($50k ×2), likely an original+amendment duplicate |

The amounts cross-validate cleanly (AT&T $300k = $300k; Microsoft $125k = $125k; Pfizer $1M = $1M).

**B. Federal lobbying registrants that gave but did NOT disclose a building gift — 13 donors, ~$2,122,500**

Altria $300k · Metropolitan Life (MetLife) $300k · Verizon $300k · Brown-Forman $300k · Boeing (via its PAC)
$250k · Beam Suntory $200k · Sazerac $150k · Comcast $100k · Churchill Downs $100k · Airbnb $50k · National
Thoroughbred Racing Assn. $50k · Rescare/BrightSpring $12.5k · Toyota $10k.

Each was confirmed to be an active federal LD-203 filer with **zero** building-fund honorary items in any
year. Two of them *did* disclose *other*, McConnell-specific honoraria on their LD-203s — **Airbnb** reported
$1,500 to the American Enterprise Institute in his honor, and **Boeing** reported $50,000 to a Hudson
Institute event in his honor — yet left the far larger building-fund gift off.

**C. Not federal lobbying filers, so no federal disclosure duty — 6 lines, ~$829,145**

Including the project's **second-largest donor overall, NWO Resources ($500,000)**, a privately held company
with no federal lobbying footprint in the data; the horse-racing names Jockey Club ($100k) and Keeneland
($100k); an individual's trust ($100k); an individual attorney ($10k); and 16 small gifts ($19,145).

**The headline, stated conservatively:** of the ~$3.60M that came from entities which file federal lobbying
reports, **$1.475M (4 donors) appears in the federal honorary record and ~$2.12M (13 donors) does not.** The
three buckets sum exactly to the $4,426,645 state total. Looking at just the first quarter reported (as an
earlier pass did) understated this badly — it looked like 3 non-disclosers / $500k; across the full period
it's 13 / ~$2.1M.

## Part 3 — The legal read (statute, actually read)

**Verdict: genuinely unclear — leaning "disclosure appears to have been required" — a question for a lawyer,
not a violation.** The 4-vs-13 split is itself what makes it live: peer companies looked at the identical gift
and split on whether to report it.

**Governing provision:** 2 U.S.C. § 1604(d)(1)(E), added by the Honest Leadership and Open Government Act of
2007; definitions at § 1602(4),(12). Read at law.cornell.edu, as-of 2026-07-15. Applied to the 13 registrants:

1. **Covered filer?** Yes — all 13 are active federal LDA registrants filing LD-203s in this period.
2. **Funds contributed in the period?** Yes — established by the state record's dated entries.
3. **To an entity "named for," or given "in recognition of," a covered official** (§ 1604(d)(1)(E)(ii))? The
   official prong is met — McConnell is a "Member of Congress," i.e., a "covered legislative branch official."
   The **crux** is the "named for / in recognition of" language: the *building* is the "Mitch McConnell
   Building," but the *recipient entity* is the "RPK Building Fund," not literally named for him. Four peers
   read this as reportable and filed it; a filer could argue (E) reaches only a gift to an entity literally
   named for the official.
4. **FEC exception?** Does not apply — (E) exempts money given to an FEC filer, but this fund reports to the
   **Kentucky** Registry, not the FEC. (Consistently, the small ordinary FECA party gifts these same companies
   made *are* FEC-reported and correctly stay out of the honorary bucket.)

So all 13 are covered filers, the money moved, the recipient isn't an FEC filer, and the building bears a
sitting senator's name. If (E)(ii) reaches a gift to a fund for a building named for a senator — as four peers
concluded — the other 13 had the same duty and didn't file. If it reaches only gifts to an entity *literally*
named for the official, none were required (and the four over-disclosed). That one interpretive question
decides it.

## Honest caveats and entity-identity notes

- **Not a violation.** "Absent from the federal honorary record" is not proof a filing was required or
  skipped. The strongest supportable statement is: *this money appears in the state record but not the federal
  honorary record, and whether the law required disclosure turns on an unsettled reading of § 1604(d)(1)(E).*
- **Entity-identity off-ramps (the most likely way a specific donor's "duty" dissolves):**
  - **MetLife** — the state donor is "Metropolitan Life Insurance Co."; the federal registrant is "MetLife
    Group, Inc." Possibly different corporate entities; the check-writer may not be the registrant.
  - **Boeing** — the gift came via "The Boeing Company PAC," not the company directly. Here the statute cuts
    *toward* a duty: (E) expressly covers a "political committee established or controlled by" the registrant.
  - **Rescare** — now "BrightSpring Health Services"; small amount ($12,500).
- **Timing.** The most recent tranches (e.g., Altria's Q4-2025 gift) fall in the H2-2025 LD-203 period due
  Jan 30, 2026; but these donors show *zero* building items across *all* years, so earlier tranches went
  undisclosed regardless.
- **Delta** is counted as disclosed at its $50k state amount; it appears as $100k federally (a likely
  duplicate), which is why the federal honorary building total ($1.525M) exceeds the disclosed state amount
  ($1.475M). Neither figure is inflated in this summary.
- **Say-vs-do context, not proof:** naming that two of the 13 disclosed *other* McConnell honoraria uses only
  the two items (Airbnb/AEI, Boeing/Hudson) whose honoree field is McConnell specifically; a Toyota Kennedy
  Center gift that lists McConnell among ~14 bipartisan honorees was **excluded** as not "in his honor."
- **No one has been contacted.** Before publication, a reporter should seek comment from the 13 registrants
  that didn't disclose, the four that did, and the Republican Party of Kentucky and Sen. McConnell's office;
  a lawyer should review the § 1604(d) reading. RPK's spokesman has said the fundraising "was done by the book."
