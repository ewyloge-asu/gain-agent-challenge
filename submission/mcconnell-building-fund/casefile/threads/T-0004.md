## Hypothesis (confirmed as a documented pattern)
Beyond capped FECA campaign contributions, lobbying registrants disclose a second, far less-scrutinized
money channel on their semiannual LD-203 reports: "Honorary Expenses" — money given to charities,
foundations, buildings, archives, and honoring events tied to specific members of Congress. This
channel is uncapped by statute, flows from the biggest corporate lobbying interests, and concentrates
around (a) the caucus-affiliated nonprofits and (b) a handful of individual power-center members.

## What's documented (see F-005..F-007)
- Scale: 4,291 honorary items, 2022-2026 Q1, totaling ~$134.4M reported; steady ~$30-38M/year (F-005).
- The giver is the registrant (contributor field is almost always "SELF"). Top registrant-givers are
  Amazon $7.16M, State Farm $5.94M, Meta $5.40M, AARP $3.86M, Comcast $3.44M, Toyota, Morgan Stanley,
  Chevron, Google (F-005).
- Biggest destinations cluster in caucus nonprofits: Congressional Black Caucus Foundation ~$25.5M
  from 101 distinct corporate funders; Congressional Hispanic Caucus Institute + Leadership Institute
  ~$22.7M from 80 funders; APAICS ~$5.0M from 39 funders (F-005).
- Individual power-center concentration: the McConnell cluster (F-006) and the single largest item of
  all — State Farm $2.65M to Habitat for Humanity "in honor of" Rep. Virginia Foxx (F-007).

## Legal status (checking-the-law, 2 U.S.C. 1604(d))
NOT a violation. HLOGA (Pub. L. 110-81 sec. 203, 2007) added 1604(d)(1)(E), which *requires*
disclosure of exactly these payments — (i) costs of an event honoring a covered official; (ii) money
to an entity named for, or given in recognition of, a covered official; (iii) money to an entity the
official established/finances/maintains/controls; (iv) costs of an event held in a covered official's
name — but sets NO dollar cap and only a $200 threshold appears elsewhere (political contributions,
subpar. D). The exception routes anything FEC-reportable to the FECA bucket instead, so these items
are by construction the *non-campaign* money. The filer certifies it did not give a gift that would
break House/Senate gift rules. So the channel is legal-by-design and disclosed; the story is the
channel + concentration, not illegality. A quid-pro-quo/gift-rule/gratuity question (18 U.S.C. 201,
1346; chamber gift rules) would only arise if facts showed personal benefit or a linked official act —
NOT provable from these filings, and lawyers'-territory only.
