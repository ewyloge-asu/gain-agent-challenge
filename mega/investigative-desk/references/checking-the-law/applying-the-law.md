# Applying a provision to facts — the deep craft

How to dissect a legal provision and test conduct against it, once you have the text in front of
you. Domain-independent. This is the discipline behind step 4 of the method; read it when the
provision is long, layered, or the verdict feels close.

## Decompose into elements
Rewrite the provision as a checklist of conditions that must ALL hold. Number them. A typical
prohibition has the shape: *[covered actor] shall not [conduct verb] [object] [in circumstances]
[within time window] [unless exception]*. Every bracket is an element; your facts must be tested
against each one separately, in writing. If you cannot decompose it cleanly, you have not
understood it yet — slow down, or the verdict will be vibes.

Test the weakest element first: verdicts are usually decided by the one element that clearly
fails (or clearly holds), not by the average of all of them.

## Definitions decide most questions
Statutes define their nouns, and the defined meaning routinely differs from the everyday one — a
"contribution" may exclude what looks like one; an "employee" may include contractors; "waters"
may or may not include a wetland; a "lobbying contact" may exclude mere strategy work. **Always
read the definitions section before the operative text**, and quote the definition in your output
when coverage turns on it. If a key term is NOT defined in the statute, check (in order): the
implementing regulations, the enforcing body's guidance, then controlling case law.

## Quantifiers: thresholds, time windows, counting rules
Many duties switch on only past a number: a dollar threshold, a percentage of time, an employee
head-count, an emissions quantity, a holding period. Three traps:
- **Aggregation rules** — thresholds often sum across a period or across affiliates ("in the
  aggregate," "per calendar year," "together with any political committee it controls"). Whether
  items aggregate is often the whole case.
- **Window anchoring** — "within one year of leaving office" anchors to a departure date you must
  establish from evidence, not assume. State the anchor date and its source explicitly.
- **Indexed amounts** — some limits adjust for inflation; the number in the statute text may not
  be the current number. The enforcing body publishes the current figure.

## State-of-mind words (and why journalists misread them)
Words like *knowingly, willfully, corruptly, intentionally, recklessly, negligently* are elements
too, and they are usually the HARDEST to satisfy — documents rarely prove state of mind. Practical
consequences for a reporting assessment:
- If the provision requires willfulness, disclosed conduct alone almost never supports
  "possible-violation"; say the objective elements are met but the mental element is unknowable
  from records.
- Civil/administrative versions of the same duty often need no mental state at all — check whether
  a strict-liability or negligence-standard sibling provision exists; your verdict may differ by
  layer.
- Never infer intent from the pattern you found. That inference is exactly what the
  investigative-method skill forbids.

## Exceptions, exemptions, and safe harbors
The operative sentence is bait; the exceptions are the fish. They live in later subsections,
cross-referenced sections, or the regulations. Work them explicitly:
1. List every "except," "does not apply," "other than," and "provided that" attached to the
   provision.
2. For each, test whether your facts fit — an exception the subject COULD have relied on matters
   even if you cannot prove they did.
3. Safe harbors (procedural routes that immunize conduct) deserve special care: if the subject
   plausibly followed one, the story is dead or different.
Symmetrically: an exemption that ALMOST fits but fails on one element can be the story ("they
structured it to look like X, but X requires Y").

## Currency: amendments, effective dates, sunset clauses
Confirm the text you read governed AT THE TIME of the conduct. Provisions are amended; conduct is
judged under the version in force when it happened. Check the amendment notes under the section:
if the law changed inside your investigation window, split the analysis into before/after regimes.
Also check for sunset clauses and delayed effective dates ("applies to contracts entered after…").

## Limitations periods
Old conduct may be beyond any enforcement window (often ~5 years for federal crimes, varying
widely for civil/administrative). This does not kill the STORY — "too old to prosecute" is still
publishable and sometimes is the story — but it changes the framing and the for-the-panel note.

## The five layers of law (and reading case law properly)
| Layer | Binding force | Verdict language |
|---|---|---|
| Statute | binding on everyone it covers | "may violate [statute]" |
| Regulation | binding; implements the statute | "may violate [rule]" |
| Agency guidance / advisory opinions | persuasive; how the enforcer will read it | "contrary to [agency] guidance" |
| Internal/chamber/professional rules | binding within the body only | "may breach [body]'s rules" — never "illegal" |
| Case law | controls how the words are read | cite the holding when a term is court-defined |

Say WHICH layer your verdict is about. "Violated House rules" and "violated federal law" are
different stories with different stakes.

Reading a case when one controls a term: identify the **holding** (the rule the court actually
needed to decide the case) and don't quote **dicta** (asides) as law. Check the court's level —
a Supreme Court holding controls everywhere; a circuit decision controls only its circuit, and a
**circuit split** means the answer genuinely differs by geography (that is a legitimate
"genuinely-unclear" verdict, and often a story). Confirm the case hasn't been overruled (the
enforcing body's current guidance usually reflects this; CourtListener shows citing cases).

## Enforcement reality — who enforces, what actually happens
A complete legal check answers three practical questions beyond "is it covered":
1. **Who can act?** Criminal prosecutor, civil regulator, administrative office, private
   plaintiffs, an ethics committee — sometimes several, sometimes effectively nobody.
2. **What are the consequences on paper?** Criminal penalties vs civil fines vs administrative
   correction ("file a late amendment") differ by orders of magnitude — calibrate the story's
   stakes to them.
3. **Is it actually enforced?** Some duties are enforced constantly, some almost never. A pattern
   of open non-compliance with an unenforced law is a *systemic* story ("a transparency law with
   no teeth") — different from, and sometimes better than, a violation story. Look for the
   enforcer's past actions (press releases, case lists) to gauge this.

## Writing about legality without getting sued
The output of this skill feeds publication, so the wording rules are part of the law-check:
- **Assert facts, question legality.** "Records show X did not file Y" (fact, verifiable) +
  "experts say this may implicate [provision]" (question) — never "X broke the law."
- Use the calibrated vocabulary exactly: **not-a-violation / possible-violation /
  genuinely-unclear.** "Possible" is the ceiling; certainty is for courts.
- Distinguish the actor from the conduct: "the filing omitted…" is safer and more precise than
  "the company concealed…" (concealment asserts intent).
- Right of reply is not optional on legal questions — the subject may hold an exemption, an
  advisory opinion, or corrected filings you cannot see. Ask specifically: "what is your view of
  [provision]'s application to [conduct]?"
- If the verdict is not-a-violation, say so prominently in the finding. Publishing the cleared
  question with the answer is honest journalism; hinting at illegality you checked and cleared is
  the defamation pattern.

## Common failure modes
- **Quoting the news summary of the law** instead of the law. Always Tier 1.
- **Stopping at the first plausible provision** — a sibling section (civil vs criminal; state vs
  federal) may fit better and flip the verdict.
- **Skipping the definitions**, then arguing about the everyday meaning of a defined term.
- **Treating registration/reporting duties as bans** — much disclosure law permits the conduct
  and only requires telling someone; the violation, if any, is the silence, not the act.
- **Ignoring the version-in-force problem** on conduct that spans an amendment.
- **Letting the pattern's ugliness leak into the legal verdict.** Newsworthiness is the beat
  skill's judgment; this skill answers only what the text covers.
