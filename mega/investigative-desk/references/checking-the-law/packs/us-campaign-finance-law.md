# EXAMPLE domain law pack #2: US campaign finance

*Second worked example of a domain law pack (see the skill's "Building a law pack" section; format:
topic → governing provision → key defined terms → scope limits/exceptions → official guidance).
Orientation only — always read the current text; limits are inflation-indexed and the FEC publishes
current figures.*

**Enforcing bodies:** Federal Election Commission (civil; `fec.gov` — advisory opinions, current
limits, enforcement matters "MURs") and DOJ Public Integrity (criminal, for knowing-and-willful
violations). State elections money is governed by 50 separate state regimes with sharply different
rules — always check the state registry too (state-level corporate money may be legal where
federal-level would not be).

## Contribution limits & source bans
- **Limits — 52 U.S.C. § 30116:** per-election caps on contributions to candidates, parties, PACs;
  amounts are indexed — get current figures from the FEC, not the statute text.
- **Corporate/union treasury ban — 52 U.S.C. § 30118:** corporations/unions cannot contribute
  treasury money to federal candidates/parties; they act through **separate segregated funds**
  (PACs) funded by employee contributions. *Scope limit:* after *Citizens United* (2010) the ban
  does NOT reach **independent expenditures** — treasury money may fund super PACs/ads made
  independently of campaigns.
- **Foreign nationals — 52 U.S.C. § 30121:** total ban on foreign-national money in ANY US
  election (federal, state, local) — contributions, expenditures, or decision-making roles.
  *Trap:* US subsidiaries of foreign parents may run PACs under strict conditions (FEC guidance);
  the line is foreign-national funds/decisions.
- **Straw donors — 52 U.S.C. § 30122:** contributions "in the name of another" are flat-out
  banned — the classic reimbursed-employee scheme; frequently criminally enforced.

## Reporting & disclosure
- **52 U.S.C. § 30104:** committees report receipts/disbursements to the FEC; itemization
  thresholds apply (donors over $200/yr). *Cross-regime note:* entities that are NOT federal
  committees may owe disclosure elsewhere (e.g., lobbying registrants' LD-203 duties under
  2 U.S.C. § 1604 — see the us-influence pack); the SAME payment can be reportable in one regime
  and invisible in another, and the mismatch can itself be the story.
- **"Dark money" boundary:** 501(c)(4) social-welfare groups spend on politics without donor
  disclosure so long as politics isn't their "primary activity" (IRS side) and their ads avoid
  FECA triggers; disclosure attaches to **electioneering communications** and express advocacy
  (52 U.S.C. §§ 30101(20), 30104(f)).

## Soft money & party funds
- **BCRA soft-money ban — 52 U.S.C. § 30125:** national parties may not raise/spend non-federal
  ("soft") money; state parties face restrictions for "Federal election activity." *Scope limits
  that matter:* what counts as Federal election activity is defined (§ 30101(20)) — and money for
  purposes OUTSIDE it (e.g., a state party's building under state law) can lawfully be raised
  from sources and in amounts federal law would forbid. Building-fund and similar carve-outs are
  the classic side door: legal at state level, invisible at federal level.

## Coordination
- **11 C.F.R. Part 109:** an "independent" expenditure coordinated with a campaign becomes an
  in-kind **contribution** (limit-capped, source-restricted). The FEC applies a three-prong test
  (payment / content / conduct). *Reality check:* coordination is notoriously under-enforced —
  a pattern of common vendors/staff between a super PAC and a campaign is usually a
  systemic-weakness story, not a provable violation.

## Personal use
- **52 U.S.C. § 30114(b):** campaign funds cannot be converted to personal use ("irrespective
  test": would the expense exist irrespective of the candidacy?). Frequent enforcement area;
  leadership-PAC spending is a well-known softer-rules gap.

## Known traps for this beat
- Statutory dollar figures are stale — the FEC's current-limits page is the number.
- "PAC" is ambiguous (traditional PAC vs super PAC vs leadership PAC vs hybrid) — the rules differ
  by type; identify which entity type you're looking at before applying any limit.
- State ≠ federal: corporate money illegal federally may be legal into state races or state-party
  non-federal accounts — jurisdiction decides the verdict (see finding-the-law.md).
- Criminal exposure generally requires **knowing and willful** conduct (§ 30109(d)) — from records
  alone, cap verdicts at possible-violation of the civil provision.
