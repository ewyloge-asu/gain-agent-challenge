# Finding the governing law — for any question, any beat

How to go from "is this even legal?" to the exact provision to read, when you don't already have a
citation. Domain-independent. (If your investigation ships a domain law pack, check it first as a
shortcut map — then still follow steps 4–5 on the real text.)

## Step 1: Characterize the conduct
Reduce the lead to one sentence of the form: *"[actor with what role] did/failed-to-do [conduct]
involving [money/asset/information] in [context]."* The **role** matters as much as the conduct —
many laws only bind defined categories (a "registrant," a "covered official," an "issuer," a
"permittee"), and role is usually where coverage is decided.

## Step 2: Fix the jurisdiction
Which government's law governs? Federal vs. state vs. local vs. foreign — often more than one at
once (our own casework hit a payment that was legal under a state statute while raising a federal
disclosure question; the two-regime overlap WAS the story). If two jurisdictions touch the
conduct, check both and say so. For non-US jurisdictions the same method applies: find the
official gazette / consolidated-legislation site for that country and its enforcing agency.

## Step 3: Find the enforcing body — "who would punish this?"
The fastest route to the right law is institutional, not textual: ask which agency, regulator,
prosecutor, or internal ethics body would act if the conduct were unlawful. That body's website
almost always has a "laws we enforce" / "statutes and regulations" page and official guidance with
citations. Examples of the move (any domain): securities conduct → the securities regulator;
pollution/permits → the environmental agency; workplace/wage conduct → the labor department;
election money → the election commission; legislators' conduct → the chamber's ethics committee;
government contracting → the procurement regulator / inspector general.

## Step 4: Go from topic to citation
In rough order of efficiency (US; analogues exist elsewhere):
1. **The enforcing body's own guidance** (step 3) — usually cites chapter and verse.
2. **Legislative research services** — for US federal, CRS reports (`crsreports.congress.gov`)
   are neutral, current, and citation-dense; search the topic in plain words.
3. **Targeted search against a primary-law site** — `site:law.cornell.edu <topic words>`, or the
   LII **Wex** legal encyclopedia for the doctrine name, then follow its citations.
4. **State law** — the state legislature's official code site; Justia/FindLaw state-code mirrors
   for navigation (verify on the official site). Local ordinances: Municode / American Legal.
5. **A domain law pack**, if your bundle has one — a pre-built topic→citation map for the beat.
Then confirm you have the *right* provision by checking its definitions cover your actor and
conduct (step 1's role test).

## Step 5: Read the right LAYER of law
A question is often governed by several layers with different force. Identify which you're reading:
| Layer | What it is | Where |
|---|---|---|
| Statute | what the legislature enacted | U.S. Code / state code |
| Regulation | the agency's binding implementation | CFR/eCFR / state admin code |
| Official guidance | how the enforcer interprets it | agency manuals, advisory opinions |
| Internal rules | bodies policing themselves (ethics rules, exchange rules) | the body's own manual |
| Case law | how courts have narrowed/read it | court opinions |
A pattern can violate an internal rule but no statute, or vice versa — say which layer your
verdict is about. If a landmark case defines a key term, read the case's holding, not a summary.

## Where law usually lives, by question type
| Investigative question | Usual home (US) |
|---|---|
| "Did they have to disclose/report this?" | the disclosure regime's own act + the filing office's guidance |
| "Is this payment/gift to an official legal?" | ethics rules of the official's body + criminal bribery/gratuity statutes |
| "Was this hire/departure improper?" | post-employment ("revolving door") statutes + the body's ethics rules |
| "Is this financial conduct legal?" | securities/banking acts + the regulator's rules |
| "Can they dump/build/emit this?" | environmental statutes + permits (the permit terms ARE law for the permittee) |
| "Is this employment practice legal?" | labor statutes + wage/hour regulations, often state-level |
| "Is this campaign money legal?" | election law (federal AND state — limits differ sharply) |
| "Did they rig this contract?" | procurement statutes/regulations + fraud statutes |

## Pitfalls
- **Right topic, wrong actor:** the law exists but doesn't bind this person/entity — check the
  definitions ("covered person," thresholds) before concluding anything.
- **Repealed/amended text:** always confirm currency on an official source; note effective dates.
- **Rule vs. law confusion:** news often says "illegal" for what is an internal-rules violation.
- **The exemption you didn't read:** scope limits and exceptions usually sit in a later subsection
  or a different section entirely — read the whole provision, not the quoted sentence.
- **Two overlapping regimes:** conduct can be legal under the regime everyone looks at and
  reportable under one nobody does — checking only one is how stories get missed.
