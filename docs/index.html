Case Agent

It works the case. People write the story.

A toolkit that lets an AI coding agent run a records investigation the way a newsroom would: gather the documents, find the leads, keep the investigation organized across sessions, and tie every claim to a source record a human can check.

We built it for the challenge's federal lobbying corpus, where it surfaced the findings below: where lobbyist money concentrates, a gap between two foreign-influence disclosure regimes, and $80 million in claimed "lobbying income" with no lobbying behind it. The workflow is packaged as Agent Skills that other teams can install and point at their own records.

One boundary is built in: the agent does not write the story. It does the records work — gathering, cross-referencing, sourcing, tracking — and hands a reporter material they can verify. News judgment and the writing stay with people.

01 · The tool

What it is

Six Agent Skills that work as one pipeline, following the same four steps a careful reporter would:

1 · Scope the questionThe agent interviews you: what's the question, which entities, what time frame, what counts as a finding? That scope becomes the case file's brief and picks the right tools for the beat. Click for details +
What's actually happening
  • Your answers become two small files: a plain-English brief — the investigation's charter — and a settings file that points the rest of the tools at your beat.
  • Lobbying comes pre-configured. Any other beat gets a starter plan built from your answers: what to treat as an entity, where to look for outside data, what counts as unusual.
  • If the data later suggests a bigger or smaller story, the agent asks again, and the change is written down.
Click to flip back
2 · Gather & analyzeDeterministic scripts load the records into a database, resolve messy names to real entities, profile what's normal, and surface what isn't — including searching the web for outside datasets and APIs to check claims against. Click for details +
What's actually happening
  • Scripts read the raw records once and organize them into a small database on your computer — a year of federal lobbying filings takes about 15 seconds.
  • Name cleanup connects the variations — "Rep. Brett Guthrie," "GUTHRIE, BRETT," "Guthrie for Congress" — to the same real person, so the money adds up correctly.
  • A data-finder suggests public datasets and official sources for your topic, and anything you pull in is saved with a digital fingerprint so it can be re-checked later.
Click to flip back
3 · Stay organizedA case file on disk tracks every thread (open, chasing, confirmed, or dead), every key entity, and a session journal — so next week's session picks up exactly where this one stopped. Click for details +
What's actually happening
  • The case file is a folder of ordinary text files — a brief, one file per lead, a list of who matters, a diary of what happened each session. You can read or edit any of it without special software.
  • Built-in rules keep it honest: a lead can't be marked confirmed without a source record behind it, and a dead end has to say why it died.
  • One command turns the whole case file into a dashboard page an editor can open in any browser.
Click to flip back
4 · Harden every claimBefore anything goes to an editor: read the actual statute and apply it element-by-element, footnote the draft to primary sources as tracked changes, and flag what still needs a human decision. The tool prepares claims for review; publishing judgments belong to people. Click for details +
What's actually happening
  • The legal checker quotes the actual statute and walks through it requirement by requirement. The strongest thing it will ever say is "possible — ask a lawyer."
  • The footnoter attaches each claim in a draft to its source, as a tracked change in Word — and where it can't find support, it says so in a margin note rather than inventing one.
  • Any number, anywhere in the work, can be traced back to the exact government filing it came from with one command.
Click to flip back

Underneath all four steps, one rule: every number carries a provenance ID that traces back to the raw filing it came from, so an editor can audit any claim in one click.

02 · The journalism

What we found

The headline: lobbyist money pools around the members of Congress who control whether legislation moves. In 2025, the top recipient of contributions reported by registered lobbyists was Rep. Brett Guthrie — $694,295 from 177 different lobbying firms — and he chairs House Energy & Commerce, the committee most corporate legislation must pass through. The rest of the leaderboard reads the same way: Ways & Means chair Jason Smith ($674,775), Appropriations chair Tom Cole, Financial Services chair French Hill, plus the floor leaders of both parties. Checked against each member's FEC totals, lobbyist money runs 13–18% of the entire campaign haul for the money-committee chairs. We claim the pattern, not causation — and we verified every recipient against the official congressional roster.

Not everything an investigation produces carries the same weight, and a panel spot-checks claims first — so each item below is labeled with how far we'd stand behind it, the same statuses the case file enforces internally:

What we foundHow much weight it bears
Money finds the gatekeepers — lobbyist contributions concentrate on committee chairs and floor leaders (the story above) Verified — every number checked against outside records and traceable to a specific filing
A foreign-influence blind spot — as Tencent fought a Pentagon designation, its U.S. lobbying spending jumped five-fold ($800K → $4.04M), and the former White House official lobbying for it appears nowhere in the foreign-agents registry. All of it lawful; the finding is that the lighter of two disclosure regimes permits exactly this Verified — same standard; the legal read is a written element-by-element check
No one validates the filings — a Delaware filer reported $80 million of "lobbying income" that matches no real lobbying: the filings themselves describe pseudo-legal reparations claims and a self-issued currency, filed as four $20M quarters — enough to make it the single biggest "client" in the 2025 data. Whatever the filer's intent, the system accepted it without question Referred to the panel — a possible misuse of a federal system, raised as a question for lawyers, not an accusation
Talking-point tracing — a detector that spots when many congressional offices push identical language in a tight window (it caught 9 offices from both parties reusing one think-tank statistic within 2 days) New capability — a reusable detection tool other teams can lift, more than a single story

And one lead we checked and closed on purpose: do lobbying firms report different dollars to the House than to the Senate for the same work? Across 36,643 matched filings — zero meaningful gaps. That negative result is recorded in the case file with its evidence, because an investigation's dead ends are knowledge too: the next reporter (or the same agent, next session) sees it was already run down and doesn't burn a week re-chasing it.

03 · The skills

The tools

An Agent Skill is a folder of instructions and scripts that a coding agent (Claude Code, Cursor, Cowork) picks up automatically. We ship the same six tools two ways — pick one:

Six separate skills

investigative-desk-menu.zip · "the menu"

  • Each skill installs, validates, and works on its own — take just the footnoter, or just the case file, into your existing setup.
  • Your agent sees six specialized tools and picks the right one per task — triggering is more precise.
  • Best for: newsrooms adopting a capability or two, and anyone who wants to mix these with their own skills.
⬇ Download the menu bundle

One skill with everything inside

investigative-desk-mega.zip · "the mega"

  • The same scripts, packaged behind a single front door (investigative-desk) — one folder to install, one skill for your agent to trigger.
  • The agent routes internally to the right tool; you don't manage six folders.
  • Best for: a fast start, demos, and installing the whole workflow at once.

04 · Step by step

Install & run

You need Python 3 and either a code-executing AI agent or just a terminal. Pick your setup:

Two things to know first.
The data: the scripts read the corpus from a folder you point to with the GAIN_DATA_DIR environment variable — that's the data/ folder Northwestern sent to registered teams and evaluators. Don't have the corpus? Skip that step entirely: every skill ships a small built-in demo dataset and runs out of the box.
API keys: none are needed to reproduce our findings. The outside data they rely on — FEC campaign totals, committee rosters, the foreign-agents registry — ships with the skills as saved, checksummed snapshots. Free keys (FEC, Congress.gov) are only for refreshing those numbers live; setup_keys.py walks you through it.
Claude Code (desktop app or the claude CLI)
  1. Download the menu bundle and unzip it: unzip investigative-desk-menu.zip -d paper-trail
  2. Copy the six skill folders into Claude Code's skills directory:
    cp -r paper-trail/skills/* ~/.claude/skills/
    (for one project only: .claude/skills/ inside that project instead)
  3. If you have the corpus, tell the scripts where it is before launching:
    export GAIN_DATA_DIR=/path/to/data — otherwise skip this.
  4. Open Claude Code and ask: "Use the investigative-method skill to reproduce the pay-the-gavel finding" — or start your own investigation: "Use investigative-method to investigate [your question] in [your data]."
Claude Cowork
  1. Download and unzip the menu bundle (or the single-skill mega).
  2. Add the unzipped folder to your Cowork workspace (drag it in, or place it in the folder Cowork is working from).
  3. Cowork sessions with code execution can run every script. If the corpus lives on your machine, mention its path in your first message.
  4. Ask: "Read paper-trail/skills/investigative-method/SKILL.md and follow it to start an investigation."
Cursor & other agents that support Agent Skills
  1. Download and unzip the menu bundle.
  2. Place the skill folders in your agent's skills directory (Cursor: ~/.cursor/skills/), or simply open the unzipped folder as part of your workspace.
  3. Works in any agent even without skills support — the universal fallback is one instruction: "Read skills/investigative-method/SKILL.md and follow it." The SKILL.md files are the manual; the agent takes it from there.
No agent — plain terminal

The deterministic pipeline runs by itself. From the unzipped bundle:

# point at the corpus (SKIP this line to use the built-in demo data)
export GAIN_DATA_DIR=/path/to/data
export GAIN_WORKDIR=$PWD/workdir

# build the database (about 15 seconds on the full 2025 corpus)
python3 skills/lobbying-influence-mapper/scripts/ingest.py --years 2025 --datasets senate contributions press
python3 skills/lobbying-influence-mapper/scripts/resolve_entities.py

# the pay-the-gavel leaderboard — Guthrie $694,295 at the top
python3 skills/lobbying-influence-mapper/scripts/xref.py gatekeeper --filer lobbyist --year 2025 --top 12

# Loc Nation's $80M flagged as the #1 outlier
python3 skills/lobbying-influence-mapper/scripts/xref.py anomaly --year 2025 --factor 5

# find outside datasets/APIs for any topic
python3 skills/investigative-method/tools/find_data.py "your topic"

06 · Generality

Not just this corpus

Nothing in the workflow is hardwired to lobbying. To check that, we ran the whole pipeline on a different question — "Which hospitals show billing patterns consistent with Medicare up-coding?" — with no lobbying-specific code involved:

1 · Scoped the new beatThe scoping step took the healthcare question and generated a healthcare investigation plan — instead of falling back to lobbying.
2 · Found & kept the dataThe data-search tool ranked CMS first, queried a live open-data portal, and surfaced the HHS Inspector General's up-coding report — then snapshotted the report and three live CMS data slices with checksums.
3 · Profiled & found leads4,079 hospital billing rows profiled; per-provider highest-severity billing shares computed against the national baseline (median 74.4%) — top outliers bill 90–92%, exactly the pattern the OIG flags for up-coding review.
4 · Flagged a data artifactThe first pass showed a wall of hospitals at exactly 100%. That turned out to be a data-suppression artifact in the CMS files, so 120 providers were excluded and the correction was recorded. The ranked leads landed in a case file with next actions.

DimensionWhere it's demonstrated
