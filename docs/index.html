Agent of Record

It works the case. People write the story.

A toolkit that lets an AI coding agent run a records investigation the way a newsroom would: gather the records, find the leads, stay organized across sessions, and tie every claim to a source record a human can check.

01 · The journalism

What we found

A request to the panel: please don't make these findings public. They haven't yet gone through our own human third-party fact-checking process or our human legal review.
  • The federal lobbying-disclosure system has no gatekeeper — a fictitious "sovereign nation" reported $80 million in lobbying fees, and nothing caught it. A single filer registered a fictitious "sovereign nation" — the "State of Loc Nation Global Public Benefit Corporation" — and reported $80 million in federal lobbying fees for 2025: more than 20 times any real client by that measure, and more than the largest real in-house lobbying budget in the country. It posted to the official public record with nothing checking it going in. The government's own auditor shows the disclosure law is enforced almost entirely against people who fail to file at all, mostly with letters and phone calls — a single $65,000 civil settlement in 2024, no criminal prosecutions — with no documented case of anyone ever penalized for filing a false figure. This is a story about the system, not the individual who filed: she may sincerely hold these beliefs, and no wrongdoing is alleged. interaction trace · the filing, live on the public record
  • Corporations with business before Congress gave $4.4 million to expand a Kentucky GOP headquarters named for Mitch McConnell — and most didn't disclose it the way federal law requires. Sixteen large corporations with business before Congress — including Pfizer, AT&T, Verizon, Boeing, Comcast, and Humana — gave a combined $4.4 million between late 2022 and 2025 to expand the Kentucky Republican Party's headquarters, a building named after then-Senate Republican Leader Mitch McConnell; a 2017 state law let them give unlimited amounts this way, sidestepping the roughly $5,000 cap on what a corporation can normally give a senator directly. Federal law separately requires any company that lobbies Congress to publicly disclose gifts made to honor a sitting member, twice a year, but only 4 of the 16 companies (Pfizer, AT&T, Microsoft, and Delta, totaling $1.475 million) reported their building-fund gifts in those federal filings; the other 12 — together giving $1.84 million, including Verizon, Boeing, Comcast, and Humana — reported nothing, even though those same companies disclosed smaller, routine gifts to the senator in the very same reports. No violation is being alleged outright, since it hinges on a genuinely disputed legal question. verification spreadsheet

02 · The skills

The tools

Six independent Agent Skills — each one works on its own. Download only the one you need, or grab all six to use together. You'll need Python 3 and Claude Code (desktop app or the claude CLI): unzip a skill into ~/.claude/skills/ (or .claude/skills/ inside one project), then try the prompt on its card. What each one does:

Start here: scope, find data, snapshot it

investigative-method

Interviews you to scope the investigation, does a quick web search to check whether the topic has already been reported on recently, finds and snapshots the outside data it needs, and renders a case file into a review dashboard. For everything else — profiling, leads, legal, footnoting — it hands off to the skill built for that job.

⬇ Download

Try: "Use the custom skill in the investigative-method folder to scope an investigation into [your question] and find outside data on [a topic]."

Click card for details +

Details

investigative-method

  • Tools: scope.py (the scoping interview → brief + beat plan, with a prior-coverage web-search check before the brief is written), find_data.py (curated catalog + live CKAN + web-search prompts), fetch_source.py (snapshot with sha256), build_dashboard.py (renders a case file into a single HTML dashboard).
  • Hands off to: case-file (organize), robodoig / lobbying-influence-mapper (profile & find leads), checking-the-law (legal), howard-center-footnoter (source a draft) — rather than reimplementing any of that here.
  • Needs: Python 3 only, stdlib. build_dashboard.py needs the case-file skill installed alongside it (reads its parser directly, no duplicate copy). Works on any dataset.

Find the leads

lobbying-influence-mapper

Loads lobbying filings, contributions, and press releases into a database; resolves messy names to real members and companies; ranks who's paying whom; checks it all against outside records (FEC, committee rosters, FARA).

Understand any table

robodoig

Point it at any CSV or table and get a profile: what the columns mean, what's normal, what's missing, what correlates. Named for Steve Doig, the Pulitzer-winning ASU data-journalism pioneer whose method it automates.

⬇ Download

Try: "Use the custom skill in the robodoig folder to profile [path/to/data.csv] — what's normal, what's missing, what stands out."

Click card for details +

Details

robodoig

  • Tools: analyze.py (types, stats, correlations, histogram + heatmap charts → one analysis.json), verify_report.py (checks every number in a written report against the JSON — a transcription fact-checker).
  • Handles: CSV/TSV/Excel, messy types, missing data, overflow values.
  • Needs: pip install pandas numpy matplotlib openpyxl.

Never lose the thread

case-file

The investigation's memory: every lead's status (open, chasing, confirmed, dead), every key entity, a session journal. Come back a week later and the agent re-orients itself instead of starting over.

⬇ Download

Try: "Use the custom skill in the case-file folder to open a case file for my investigation into [topic], and give me a re-orientation brief whenever I come back to it."

Click card for details +

Details

case-file

  • Tools: casefile.py — init, brief (the re-orientation digest), update-status, log, rollup, lint.
  • Honesty rules, enforced: confirmed requires a source record; cold requires a reason; chasing requires a next action.
  • Needs: Python 3 only. Plain files — readable and editable by humans.

Is it actually illegal?

checking-the-law

Takes a lead, pulls the real statute text, and applies it element-by-element — never from memory. Its verdicts are deliberately cautious: "possible," "unclear, ask a lawyer," or "not a violation, and here's why."

⬇ Download

Try: "Use the custom skill in the checking-the-law folder to check whether [a fact pattern] violates [a statute, e.g. the Lobbying Disclosure Act]."

Click card for details +

Details

checking-the-law

  • Method: fetch the current statute text, break it into elements, test the facts against each element, cite what's missing.
  • Law packs: reusable per-domain statute maps (LDA, FARA shipped; synthesize new ones per beat).
  • Output: a written verdict memo — the McEntee/FARA legal check in this submission is one example.

Source every claim

howard-center-footnoter

Reads a draft story, matches each factual claim to your reporting materials, and inserts Howard Center–style footnotes as Word tracked changes — with margin comments where no source exists or a quote may be out of context.

⬇ Download

Try: "Use the custom skill in the howard-center-footnoter folder to footnote my draft at [path/to/draft.docx] against my source materials in [path/to/sources]."

Click card for details +

Details

howard-center-footnoter

  • Tools: claim extraction, source ingestion (PDF/transcript/notes), footnote formatting, tracked-change insertion, source-tier checking (primary beats secondary; Wikipedia is never an endpoint).
  • Flags, not fabrications: ⟨NEEDS SOURCE⟩ placeholders + margin comments for anything unsupported.
  • Needs: pip install python-docx lxml (+ pdfplumber for PDFs).

03 · Honest edges

Limitations

  • Generalizing cost some corpus-specific sharpness. Earlier, lobbying-tuned versions surfaced certain leads more readily than the generalized skills do.
  • This is a submission-stage tool. It needs continued refinement across more datasets and more newsroom feedback before we'd call it production software.

04 · Reflections

Reflections on this competition

Most of us on this team had never built a Claude Skill before this competition, and we're genuinely glad it pushed us to learn — several of us are now using Agent Skills in other projects and in our teaching.

It was also inspiring in a broader way. We learned a lot from each other, and we'd like to organize similar cross-disciplinary competitions for students at ASU so they can have that same experience.
